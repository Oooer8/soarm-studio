Calibration principles

SOARM calibration turns raw servo encoder ticks into meaningful joint angles, safe software limits, and repeatable data. The Studio command only routes the workflow; the physical sampling and config writeback stay in soarm-sdk.

Why calibration exists

Every assembled arm has small mechanical differences: horn mounting offsets, mirrored joint direction, cable routing, and usable travel that may not match the nominal model. A servo can report a raw tick, but that tick is not yet a stable joint angle until the software knows which physical pose should be zero and which direction is positive.

Calibration stores that relationship in each arm config so later commands can reason in radians instead of raw hardware ticks.

Calibration data model text 
raw servo tick
  -> zero_tick + direction
  -> joint radians
  -> min_rad / max_rad safety envelope
  -> check, teleop, record, replay, diagnostics

What is recorded

Zero tick

The current encoder reading at the known physical zero pose. It is the reference offset for converting present position into joint radians.

Joint direction

The sign convention that keeps positive joint motion consistent with the model, even when two arms or joints are mirrored.

Min and max limits

The observed safe travel range from the manual sweep, saved as joint-space limits for checks and clipping.

Diagnostics

Pre- and post-calibration status reports help confirm the arm was reachable and that the saved limits are plausible.

How the live flow works

  1. Run check first to confirm the role config and bus are reachable.
  2. Place the selected arm in the known zero pose when prompted.
  3. The SDK captures the present raw encoder ticks as zero_tick.
  4. Torque is disabled so you can sweep each joint by hand.
  5. The SDK samples joint readings continuously and tracks observed min/max values.
  6. The updated calibration is written back to that arm config.
  7. Run check again before teleop or recording.

Why it matters

Teleoperation depends on leader and follower readings using the same joint convention. Recording depends on observations being reproducible across sessions. Safety depends on software limits matching the arm that is actually on the table. Bad calibration can make an arm look connected while its angles, limits, or mirrored signs are wrong.

!
Calibration is read-first

The live flow reads encoder positions while you move the arm by hand. It should not command motion during the zero capture or range sweep.
